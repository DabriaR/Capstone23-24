Initial Research

Problem Statement:
Stertypes are continously perpetuated because of negative portayal or lack of representation of African Americans.
Need Statement:
Media needs to have more inclusive and authentic views of race, specifically starting at younger ages so these steretypes are challenged.
Issue
The lack of black representaion in media continues to perpetuate harmful stereotypes. The underrepresentation of black individuals in forms of media such as film, television and advertising has reinforced harmful narratives and racial bias. This perpertuation of negative stereotypes dates back all the way to late 19th century, during the Jim Crow era, where stereotypes were brought to the theatrical stage by blackface ministrel. This pattern continued throughout movies in the 1900s, mammys, jezebelles, and aunt jemimah. The theme is continued today through modern stereotypes that illustrate black people as "angry" or "lazy", causing the likeliness of white people to subsequently hold these stereotypes. In addition to this, black voices are silence through forms of animated media due to white actors being picked as voice actors for african american characters which is considered as misrepresentation.
Quantative Evidence
A study within the african american community about black american experiences with news state that:
  • Almost two-thirds of Black adults (63%) say news about Black people is often more negative than news about other racial and ethnic groups; 28% say it is about equal and 7% say it is often more positive.
  • 57% say the news only covers certain segments of Black communities, compared with just 9% who say it covers a wide variety of Black people.
  • Half say coverage is often missing important information, while only 9% say it often reports the full story.
  • 43% say the coverage largely stereotypes Black people, far higher than the 11% who say it largely does not stereotype. An additional 43% say both of these things happen about equally.
The survey asked Black Americans if they ever come across news that is racist or racially insensitive about Black people in some way. About four-in-ten (39%) say they see this kind of racially problematic news extremely or fairly often, and an additional 41% say they sometimes see such news.
